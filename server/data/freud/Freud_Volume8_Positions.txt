FREUD: COLLECTED PAPERS VOLUME 8 - POSITION STATEMENTS

=== CATHARTIC METHOD ===

1. The cathartic method treats hysteria through verbal expression of repressed memories.
2. The procedure of verbal utterance was described as a 'talking cure'.
3. The patient jokingly referred to catharsis as 'chimney-sweeping'.
4. After giving utterance to hallucinations, patients lose obstinacy and regain energy.
5. Complete relief of imaginative products is essential for good therapeutic results.
6. When relief was complete, patients became calm, agreeable, and cheerful the next day.
7. On subsequent days without treatment, patients became increasingly moody and contrary.
8. The therapeutic effect of discoveries under hypnosis is immediate and lasting.
9. Symptoms can be cleared up by giving verbal utterance to repressed experiences.
10. The mind is completely relieved after reproducing frightful images and giving verbal utterance to them.

=== HYSTERIA AND SYMPTOMS ===

11. A symptom can be determined by its precipitating cause.
12. Suppressed emotions during a meal can lead to hysterical vomiting.
13. Anxiety and hallucinations can lead to paresis, contracture, and anaesthesia.
14. Severe hysteria can result in loss of one's native language.
15. The intention to remain still can paradoxically produce involuntary movements.
16. A tic can develop from an involuntary noise and persist for many years.
17. Hysterical symptoms are connected to precipitating traumatic events.
18. The connection between trauma and symptom can be evident and clear.
19. Symptoms have quite obviously been determined by precipitating causes.
20. Hysteria involves conversion of psychical excitation into physical symptoms.

=== TRAUMA AND MEMORY ===

21. Traumatic memories remain affectively charged until discharged through verbal expression.
22. The essential features of hysteria remained constant throughout observation.
23. Stories became more tragic after the father's death in the Anna O case.
24. Evening narratives changed into frightful hallucinations after mental deterioration.
25. Hysterical patients retain memories that normal individuals would forget.
26. The pathogenesis of hysteria involves incubation of symptoms over time.
27. Traumatic experiences create psychical stimuli that require discharge.
28. The mounting up and intensification of symptoms increases in evening hours.
29. Products of imagination act as psychical stimuli requiring release.
30. Removal of the stimulated state occurs when experiences are verbalized in hypnosis.

=== CASE OF ANNA O ===

31. Anna O shared duties of nursing her sick father with her mother.
32. Anna woke during the night in great anxiety about her feverish father.
33. Anna fell into a waking dream and saw a black snake approaching her father.
34. Anna's right arm became anaesthetic and paretic while trying to ward off the snake.
35. When Anna looked at her arm, the fingers turned into little snakes with death's heads.
36. Anna tried to pray but language failed her until she thought of English verses.
37. The hallucination of snakes became associated with the arm's paralysis.
38. The alternation between two states of consciousness persisted despite treatment.
39. Anna hallucinated during conversations and would run off or climb trees.
40. Analysis dealt with the preparatory period of Anna's illness in summer 1880.

=== CASE OF FRAU EMMY ===

41. Frau Emmy's stories should be heard in detail to the very end.
42. Interrupting a patient's story gains nothing therapeutically.
43. The author could not evade listening to stories in every detail.
44. Fresh crops of fears can be taken from patients through therapeutic intervention.
45. Patients should believe the therapist more than those who spread frightening stories.
46. The cause of ill-humour can be residues of stories kept back.
47. Frau Emmy hated her child for three years after her husband's death.
48. She believed she could have nursed her husband back to health if not for the child.
49. After her husband's death there was nothing but insults and agitations.
50. Her relatives spread a rumor that she poisoned her husband.

=== PSYCHICAL MECHANISMS ===

51. Hysterical counter-will produces involuntary actions opposite to intentions.
52. Two states of consciousness can alternate within the same patient.
53. Condition seconde represents an altered state of consciousness.
54. The habit of not hearing can be differentiated into several categories.
55. Disorders of vision can be cleared up through hypnotic intervention.
56. Patients in asylums were historically treated with ice-cold water douches.
57. Patients were put into apparatus that turned them round until quiet.
58. Fear of asylums can develop from hearing stories about their treatment.
59. The origin of unsociability and hatred of strangers can be traumatic persecution.
60. Neuropaths experience primary horror of animals from youth onwards.

=== HYPNOSIS AND TREATMENT ===

61. Shutting the patient's eyes at night with suggestion prevents disturbance.
62. The disturbance was only repeated when the patient cried in a dream.
63. Complete insight into the incubation and pathogenesis of hysteria can be obtained.
64. Hypnotic suggestion can prevent patients from opening their eyes until morning.
65. Analysis of symptoms deals with the preparatory period of illness.
66. Calming words about traumatic stories make patients feel easier.
67. Patients can become docile and submissive after confrontation about symptoms.
68. Patients may accept the doctor's view only because the doctor says so.
69. Inability to eat more can result from traumatic memories related to food.
70. Childhood experiences with disgusting food cause lasting eating problems.

=== BREUER'S CONTRIBUTIONS ===

71. Breuer's contributions to Studies on Hysteria should be reprinted without alteration.
72. Freud's share of the book is also reprinted without alteration.
73. Developments and changes in Freud's views were too far-reaching to attach to earlier exposition.
74. Freud does not wish to eliminate evidence of his initial views.
75. Freud regards his initial views as valuable first approximations to knowledge.
76. Full knowledge could only be acquired after long and continuous efforts.
77. Germs of all that was later added to catharsis theory can be detected in early work.
78. Psychosexual factors play a part in the theory of catharsis.
79. Infantilism is important in the development of hysteria.
80. Dreams and unconscious symbolism are important in catharsis theory.

=== FEAR AND PHOBIAS ===

81. Fear of contagion from a brother's disease affected eating habits.
82. Nursing a brother with consumption created food-related trauma.
83. Intolerance to water can stem from family experience with gastric catarrh.
84. Bad drinking water caused gastric catarrh in the family.
85. Belief that mineral water would not help persisted despite prescription.
86. Eating and drinking habits improved immediately after hypnotic session.
87. Two months after treatment, significant weight gain was reported.
88. Patients continued healthy habits after therapeutic intervention.
89. Fear of animals can be symbolic or represent primary horror.
90. Essential origin of fear of people was persecution after husband's death.

=== THERAPEUTIC INSIGHTS ===

91. Therapeutic discoveries under hypnosis have immediate and lasting effect.
92. Patients do not starve themselves after properly conducted cathartic treatment.
93. The patient reported eating excellently and gaining weight after treatment.
94. Patients continued prescribed mineral water after therapeutic breakthrough.
95. Abnormal development in children can coincide with parental treatment.
96. Normal relations between doctor and patient are most friendly.
97. Sharp contrast can occur between therapeutic confrontation and normal relations.
98. Patients can become docile after being given ultimatums about their views.
99. A series of chronologically arranged traumatic reasons underlies symptoms.
100. Traumatic memories are stored and can be accessed through therapeutic technique.

=== SELF-BLAME AND GUILT ===

101. Patients can be hard on themselves and blame themselves severely for minor neglects.
102. Minor neglects like misplaced towels cause severe self-blame in patients.
103. Patients feel sorry for betraying nervousness to the doctor.
104. Patients may greet the doctor claiming they hate themselves.
105. Fright at the appearance of visitors can be a relic of former nervousness.
106. Patients assure that alarming reactions are only occasional occurrences.
107. The patient's alarm at Dr. Breuer's visit was described as a one-time occurrence.
108. Patients hate themselves for showing residual symptoms.

=== PARANOIA AND PERSECUTION ===

109. A case of paranoia can run counter to psycho-analytic theory.
110. Husband's relatives were against the marriage due to the couple's happiness.
111. Relatives spread rumors of poisoning after husband's death.
112. Patients wanted to demand inquiry into false accusations.
113. Relatives involved patients in legal proceedings.
114. Shady journalists helped with persecution through legal means.
115. Agents were sent to stir up people against the patient.
116. Local papers printed libellous articles about the patient.
117. Cuttings of libellous articles were sent to torment the patient.
118. Persecution was the origin of unsociability and hatred of strangers.

=== CHILDHOOD AND DEVELOPMENT ===

119. Freud discusses a childhood recollection from Dichtung und Wahrheit.
120. A child being beaten is a significant psychoanalytic theme.
121. Childhood experiences with food create lasting psychological effects.
122. Naughtiness in childhood leads to forced eating of cold meat.
123. The threat of condign punishment forces children to eat unpleasant food.
124. Cold meat with set fat creates lasting disgust and eating problems.

=== SYMBOLISM ===

=== METHODOLOGY ===

139. The author gave up trying to hypnotize resistant patients.
140. Ultimatums were given to accept psychosomatic explanations.
141. Patients were threatened with being asked to leave if they did not comply.
142. Gastric pains were attributed to fear despite patient resistance.
143. Twenty-four hours were given to accept the doctor's view.
144. Confrontation was in sharp contrast to normally friendly relations.

146. The significance of animal visions was not fully investigated.
147. Symbolic and primary horror aspects of fear should be distinguished.
