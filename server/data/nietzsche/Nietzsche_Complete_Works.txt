*   **The "unhistorical" sense—the ability to forget—is as essential to health for an individual or a people as the historical sense.**

*   **The "suprahistorical" perspective, which sees the present as eternal and complete in itself, offers a counterpoint to the ceaseless becoming of historical thought.**

*   **The German spirit has been co-opted and misled by the false glitter of political power and a superficial, journalistic conception of culture.**

*   **The "cultural philistine" is the dominant type of the age: a smugly educated person who mistakes historical knowledge for culture and is blind to true greatness.**

*   **Public opinion, fueled by the fast-paced press, is a tyrannical force that enforces conformity and destroys originality and slow, serious thought.**

*   **The modern age suffers from a "personality-dissolving" force where individuals become mere points of intersection for impersonal, historical currents.**

*   **True heroes of culture are those who fight for the future, not those celebrated by the present establishment.**

*   **Imitation of past models, without a profound inner need, leads only to empty stylization and cultural epigonism (the age of the followers).**

*   **We need "untimely" figures—like Schopenhauer and Wagner—who act counter to their time and thereby exert a beneficial influence on the future.**

*   **The health of a people depends more on its ability to creatively mythologize its existence than on its political or economic achievements.**

*   **Historical knowledge, when not subordinated to a higher, life-giving goal, produces a cynical relativism that undermines all conviction and action.**

Human, All Too Human

*   **Most of what is called "metaphysical" or "religious" truth is actually the product of profound psychological errors and needs, not divine revelation.**
*   **The world of good and evil, purpose and meaning, is a strictly human world of appearances layered over a fundamentally amoral and purposeless reality.**
*   **Moral feelings are not insights into a divine order but rather inherited social habits and evaluations hardened over millennia into a sense of the absolute.**
*   **The ascetic ideal and the belief in selfless, purely "good" actions stem from a fundamental misjudgment of human motivation, which is always mixed and self-referential.**
*   **What humans call "free will" is a fiction; every action is a necessary link in a complex chain of physiological and psychological causality.**
*   **The feeling of remorse is not a voice of God but the sting of a thwarted instinct of power, the pain of having exercised one's power stupidly.**
*   **The origins of justice lie not in a sense of fairness but in the prudent self-interest of roughly equal powers seeking to avoid mutual destruction.**
*   **Pity is not an unambiguously good moral sentiment but a weakness that often increases the amount of suffering in the world by multiplying pain.**
*   **Society and morality exist not for the benefit of the individual but as a system of constraints that make communal life possible by taming and regulating the individual.**
*   **The concepts of punishment and guilt have entirely separate historical origins; punishment was originally conceived without any reference to the "guilt" of the offender.**
*   **The philosopher of the future must be a free spirit, a skeptical investigator who employs psychology and history as his primary tools to dismantle cherished illusions.**
*   **Art does not reveal higher truths but serves vital human needs for pleasure, intoxication, and the temporary adornment of life's harsh realities.**
*   **The "genius" is not a supernatural figure but a person of immense energy who, through fortunate inheritance and circumstance, manages to organize all their forces toward a single goal.**
*   **Christianity triumphed not because of its truth but because of its shrewd psychological appeal to the weak, the suffering, and the resentful masses of the Roman Empire.**
*   **The state is not a sacred institution but a violent and cruel founding act that is later legitimized by custom and forgetfulness.**
*   **The desire for metaphysical certainty and a world "behind" the appearances is a sign of sickness, not strength; health lies in accepting the world of becoming.**
*   **Custom is the mightiest tyrant over humanity, and what is called "morality" is often just the most ancient and venerable custom.**
*   **All higher culture begins with the spiritualization of cruelty, such as in tragic festivals or the demand for self-overcoming in religion and philosophy.**
*   **The belief in opposites (e.g., egoistic/altruistic) is a crude, popular error; in reality, there are only degrees and subtle nuances of mixed motives.**
*   **The drive for knowledge is born from the drive for possession and mastery; to understand something is a form of appropriation.**
*   **What men have called "love" is in fact a complex mixture of lust, the drive for possession, vanity, and the desire to be recognized.**
*   **Marriage as an institution was originally founded on economic and social necessity, not romantic love, and continues to be sustained by those forces.**
*   **The "bad conscience" is not divine but an internalization of the cruelty humans could no longer discharge outward once they were enclosed within the peace of society.**
*   **The scientific spirit is the successor to the religious spirit, offering a more modest, piecemeal, but ultimately more honest approach to understanding the world.**
*   **Democracy represents the secularization of the Christian ideal of the equal value of every soul and will lead to the flattening of human types.**
*   **The highest activity of man is not in moral action but in artistic creation and intellectual conquest, which are forms of the "will to power."**
*   **Convictions are more dangerous enemies of truth than lies because they are held with a closed mind, immune to counter-evidence.**
*   **One must have chaos within oneself to give birth to a dancing star; periods of disintegration and doubt are necessary for higher creation.**
*   **The free spirit experiments with life and thought, holding his beliefs lightly and being willing to sacrifice any conviction for a better one.**
*   **The history of humanity is the history of its errors, and progress consists not in finding truth but in replacing grosser errors with more refined and useful ones.**
*   **Rights are recognized and guaranteed degrees of power; when the balance of power shifts, old rights dissolve and new ones are established.**
*   **The concept of "punishment" contains at least ten different meanings and purposes, which have been conflated and confused over time.**
*   **Great men are like epochs; they concentrate a tremendous force and then discharge it in a single, monumental direction, for better or worse.**
*   **Vanity is the fear of appearing original, and thus the herd instinct of the individual who seeks safety in conformity and approval.**
*   **Mystical explanations are considered deep, but the truth is that they are not even superficial, as they explain nothing.**
*   **The demand for art and beauty is a demand for illusion; it is a sign of suffering from the truth and a need for its temporary veiling.**
*   **The thinker must now be the physician of culture, diagnosing the illnesses of the age and prescribing regimens for its health.**
*   **What separates higher men from the herd is their capacity for sustained, focused attention and their ability to bear the weight of responsibility.**
*   **The so-called "world order" is a human projection; the actual universe is a chaos devoid of any inherent order, beauty, or harmony.**
*   **The greatest events are not our noisiest, but our stillest hours; the most important thoughts come on dove's feet.**
*   **One must learn to love and embrace necessity; the formula for human greatness is *amor fati*: to love one's fate and not wish for anything to be different.**
*   **The knowledge-drive has now reached a point where it threatens to undermine the very foundations of life, which are built on illusion and error.**
*   **The future of humanity lies in the conscious breeding and education of a higher type of man, not in the haphazard progress of the masses.**
*   **The ultimate goal of humanity cannot lie in its end but in its highest specimens; the meaning of history is the production of great individuals.**
*   **A politician divides mankind into tools and enemies; this is all he can understand, revealing the profound intellectual poverty of the political realm.**
*   **Moral judgments are a subspecies of aesthetic judgments; they are based on feelings of pleasure and displeasure, not on rational insight.**
*   **The belief in causality is a primal superstition and habit, not a logical necessity; we have no right to the concept of "cause" as something operating in nature.**
*   **The "thing-in-itself" is a nonsensical concept; we only ever have relations, and a "thing" is merely the sum of its effects on us.**
*   **The free spirit's task is one of active nihilism: to devalue the highest values that have guided humanity thus far in order to clear the ground for new creations.**


Daybreak


*   **The primary task for modern philosophy is the critique of morality, questioning the value and origins of our most deeply held values.**
*   **All previous moral philosophy has been naive, taking morality as a given rather than as a problem to be investigated and questioned.**
*   **Our moral sensations are not divinely implanted but are historically grown, tied to errors of reason and primitive social conditions.**
*   **The distinction between "good" and "evil" is not eternal but was a late and revolutionary invention, primarily by priestly castes seeking power.**
*   **The older, aristocratic morality distinguished only between "good" and "bad," where "good" meant noble, powerful, and beautiful, and "bad" meant common, weak, and contemptible.**
*   **The morality of altruism and self-sacrifice has its psychological roots in feelings of fear, weakness, and the calculation of utility, not in pure love.**
*   **What is praised as "selflessness" is often a subtle form of egoism, vanity, or a desire to exercise power over others through dependency.**
*   **The ascetic ideal—the praise of poverty, chastity, and humility—is a profound trick of the will to power exercised by those who are physiologically weakened.**
*   **Cruelty is one of humanity's oldest and most fundamental festive joys and has been spiritualized and sanctified in countless cultural practices.**
*   **The concept of punishment did not originate to hold people responsible but as a simple, irrational outburst of anger against a cause of injury.**
*   **The feeling of "guilt" was originally just the material feeling of debt (Schuld), a contractual relation between creditor and debtor, later moralized.**
*   **The "bad conscience" is a sickness, like a pregnancy, born when humans were forcibly enclosed in the peace of society and turned their aggressive instincts inward upon themselves.**
*   **The idea of a God as the ultimate creditor, to whom we owe an unpayable debt, is the pinnacle of this moralization of indebtedness, leading to the neurosis of Christian guilt.**
*   **Our intellectual world is a pale afterimage of our affective and instinctual world; thoughts are the shadows of our feelings.**
*   **Habit is the great economizer of human strength, allowing us to function without constant conscious deliberation, but it also makes us stupid and resistant to change.**
*   **The demand for "love of one's neighbor" is often a demand for mediocrity, a flight from oneself, and a fear of the superior individual.**
*   **One must learn to be selfish in the right way—a healthy, self-affirming selfishness that cultivates one's own garden rather than a petty selfishness focused on possession.**
*   **The so-called "free will" of theologians, the idea of an uncaused cause within the soul, is an absurdity invented for the purpose of punishment and guilt.**
*   **Nothing is rarer than a personal action; most of what we do is an impersonal, inherited custom performed under the hypnosis of society.**
*   **The philosopher must be a vivisectionist of the soul, courageously dissecting moral sentiments to reveal their often-ugly, all-too-human roots.**
*   **Christianity made Eros poison by mixing it with the concepts of guilt, sin, and the afterlife, thereby spoiling the most innocent joys.**
*   **Pity, as currently practiced, is a weakness that multiplies suffering and represents a covert form of contempt for the one pitied.**
*   **The search for knowledge must be pursued with a cheerful and subtle hardness, free from the ponderous seriousness and moralizing of the religious spirit.**
*   **Virtues are as dangerous as vices when they are exercised from blind instinct and tradition rather than from conscious, personal insight.**
*   **The greatest changes in history are caused by subtle, imperceptible shifts in the weight of evaluations, not by dramatic events or wars.**
*   **The drive for knowledge originates in the drive to appropriate and master the world; to know something is to make it our own.**
*   **Modern science is the last evolutionary stage of the ascetic ideal, as it demands rigorous self-denial and discipline from its practitioners.**
*   **Morality is herd instinct in the individual; the voice of the herd speaks through us when we feel the sting of conscience.**
*   **One must have the courage to live experimentally, to be a law unto oneself, even if this means becoming evil in the eyes of the moral majority.**
*   **The belief in a moral world order, where good is rewarded and evil punished, is a childish superstition that has been refuted by every hour of human history.**
*   **The value of an action lies not in its intention but in its consequences, though these are often unforeseeable and tangled in a web of causality.**
*   **The praise of work in modern society is a clever device to keep everyone busy and prevent them from asking dangerous, fundamental questions.**
*   **Nationalism and the modern state are new idols that demand the same blind devotion once given to the Church.**
*   **What is called "objectivity" in scholars is often just a lack of personality, a fear of taking a stand and having a taste of one's own.**
*   **We must become the inventors of our own virtues, creators of new values, rather than merely repeating the old commandments like a phonograph.**
*   **The great health is the ability to overcome and integrate one's sicknesses, not to be free from them entirely.**
*   **The real philosophical harvest is reaped in the open air, during solitary walks, not in the dusty confinement of the study.**
*   **The history of morality is the history of a long, spiritual cruelty, in which humanity has sacrificed its most natural instincts on the altar of an imaginary God.**
*   **The "improvement" of mankind preached by moralists has largely been a taming, a weakening, and a making-medicore of the human animal.**
*   **Nothing has been more costly to humanity than the errors of its moralists, who have slandered the natural world and human instincts.**
*   **A thought comes when "it" wants, not when "I" want; the subject "I" is a fiction added to the process of thinking.**
*   **We must learn to think differently in order to eventually live differently; a revaluation of all values begins with a new way of thinking.**
*   **The greatest danger for the most fruitful human beings has always been the pressure of their times, the tyranny of public opinion.**
*   **To translate humanity back into nature is the great task; to master the many vain interpretations that have been scribbled over the eternal text of nature.**
*   **The free spirit is one who has freed himself from the chains of gratitude and resentment, who can view his past teachers and enemies with a clear, unclouded eye.**
*   **There is no necessary opposition between the egoistic and the altruistic; a well-understood, great egoism can benefit others more than any conscious altruism.**
*   **One must sail the seas of morality with courage, sounding the depths, discovering new coasts, and having the strength to abandon the safe harbor of tradition.**
*   **The aim of this book is to sow skepticism about morality itself, to make morality a problem, and thus to prepare for a future creation of values.**
*   **Our old moral interpretations of the world have become unbelievable; we stand before a horizon that appears empty, which is the precondition for new dawns.**

Daybreak: Thoughts on the Prejudices of Morality
Morality is not derived from divine command but is a human, all-too-human construction.

The feeling of pity is not inherently good but often a disguised form of self-enjoyment and vanity.

The ascetic ideal and self-denial originate from a misinterpretation of dreams and bad conscience, not holiness.

The Christian emphasis on purity and the condemnation of sensuality is a life-denying error.

Our moral judgments are preceded by long-forgotten errors residing in our language and customs.

The so-called "inner voice" of conscience is really the voice of social authorities we have internalized.

The drive for knowledge originated not from utility but from error, dreams, and superstition.

The belief in free will is a primary error that makes men feel guilty and accountable for their inherent nature.

Custom is the tyrant over all human society, far stronger than any individual joy or suffering.

The morality of altruism and "love of neighbor" is often a subtle flight from oneself.

The Socratic demand for rationality in all drives is itself a kind of sickness.

Punishment was not invented to bring repentance but was originally a form of compensating creditors.

We must become the poets of our lives, constructing our values from our experiences, not inherited dogma.

The saint is not a moral ideal but often a person consumed by a monstrous will to power in a distorted form.

The belief in moral opposites (good/evil) is a simplistic and erroneous interpretation of a continuum of drives.

The "bad conscience" is a sickness, like a pregnancy, that can give birth to something new under the right conditions.

The desire for certainty in religion or philosophy is a sign of weakness and fear of the flux of life.

Gratitude was originally a form of revenge, where the weaker party, by being grateful, could pretend to bestow a favor.

What we call "justice" often begins as the anger of the offended party seeking compensation.

The philosophical drive must be redirected from seeking eternal truths to interpreting the shifting perspectives of life.

Our actions are essentially unknown to us because we act from a chaos of drives we do not comprehend.

The history of morality is a history of psychological cruelty turning inward upon the self.

The "good man" of morality is frequently the end result of taming and weakening the human animal.

The free spirit must embark on a great liberation, freeing itself from the chains of customary morality.

Religious neurosis stems from a misunderstanding of natural phenomena, attributing them to angry or benevolent spirits.

The value of an action lies not in its intention but in its consequences and its role in the economy of life.

We should experiment with morality, testing values as hypotheses rather than accepting them as commandments.

The denial of the body and the senses is the denial of the fundamental ground of knowledge and life.

The concept of "sin" is an invention of priestly classes to gain power over the healthy and noble.

Courage is the ultimate virtue that allows one to attack one's own convictions and embrace uncertainty.

The fear of the unfamiliar is the root of all moral condemnation.

Philosophical systems are ultimately a confession of faith by their author, not objective truths.

The democratic movement is the heir of Christianity, spreading a herd morality that fears all exceptions.

We must learn to love ourselves, not with a shallow vanity, but with a profound health that includes our "evil" aspects.

The search for origins reveals not noble beginnings but crude, violent, and error-prone processes.

The meaning of our existence is not given; we must become the legislators who assign meaning.

Laughter and humor are essential tools for the free spirit to distance itself from the heaviness of moral dogma.

What is now called "conscience" was originally the cruelty of man unable to discharge itself outwardly.

The progress of knowledge is achieved by overcoming not error, but morality.

We should be suspicious of any morality that speaks too loudly of rewards and punishments.

The greatest events are not our noisiest, but our stillest moments of thought and revaluation.

We should not seek to escape pain and contradiction but to see them as necessary for growth.

Convictions are more dangerous enemies of truth than lies, because they are rigid and unconscious.

A thought comes when "it" wants, not when "I" want; the subject is a later addition.

The purpose of existence is not happiness but suffering, growth, and the feeling of increased power.

To see science under the optics of the artist, and art under the optics of life, is the task.

The ultimate gratitude to existence is to will its eternal return in every detail.

We have left the land of moral certainty and have embarked on the open sea; we must now be our own captains.

The greatest events of an age are its silent thoughts, not its noisy politics.

The desire for tranquility, for a "peace of soul," is often a desire for death in disguise.

One must have a predilection for fighting and victory to understand anything profound.

The world is a work of art that gives birth to itself, and we are part of its creative process.

The gay science (la gaya scienza) is the combination of a passionate, suffering, depressive nature with a intellectual, mocking, mobile mind.

Thus Spoke Zarathustra
Man is a rope stretched between the animal and the Overman—a rope over an abyss.

The Overman is the meaning of the earth; your will shall say: the Overman shall be the meaning of the earth!

I teach you the Overman: man is something that shall be overcome.

The body is a great reason, a plurality with one sense, a war and a peace, a herd and a shepherd.

The spirit goes through three metamorphoses: it becomes a camel, then a lion, and finally a child.

To create new values—that even the lion cannot do; but to create itself freedom for new creation—that the lion can do.

The child is innocence and forgetting, a new beginning, a game, a self-propelled wheel, a first movement, a sacred "Yes."

You must be ready to burn yourself in your own flame; how could you become new if you had not first become ashes?

I say unto you: one must still have chaos in oneself to give birth to a dancing star.

The Higher Men are not the goal; they are a bridge to the Overman, and they too must be overcome.

The way of the creator is to become a hermit and a law-breaker, breaking the old tablets of values.

The state is the coldest of all cold monsters; it lies when it says, "I, the state, am the people."

On the market-place nobody believes in Higher Men; you must go into solitude to speak of the Overman.

You shall love peace as a means to new wars—and the short peace more than the long.

You shall not be a fly-swatter for those who preach equality; the tarantulas of "justice" preach the equality of all.

Life is that which must always overcome itself; it is a well of delight, but also of pain and conflict.

This is my teaching: whoever wants to be a creator in good and evil must first be an annihilator and break values.

The will to power is the secret of life: all living things seek to discharge their strength and grow.

The wisest of you is only a conflict and hybrid of plant and ghost; but do I bid you become ghosts or plants?

God is a conjecture; but I desire that your conjecturing not reach beyond your creative will.

If there were gods, how could I bear not to be a god! Therefore there are no gods.

Remain faithful to the earth, my brothers, and do not believe those who speak of otherworldly hopes!

The belly of being does not speak to humans at all, except as a human.

The best images and parables should speak of time and becoming; they should be a eulogy and justification of all impermanence.

The will cannot will backwards; that is its most lonely torment.

The spirit of revenge, which calls itself "justice," is the will's ill will against time and its "it was."

To redeem the past and to re-create all "it was" into a "thus I willed it"—that alone should I call redemption.

This, yes this alone, is revenge itself: the will's antipathy toward time and time's "it was."

All "it was" is a fragment, a riddle, a dreadful accident—until the creative will says to it, "But thus I willed it!"

The overman is the sea in which your great contempt can drown.

Man is something that must be overcome: he is a bridge and not a purpose.

You great star, what would your happiness be if you had not those for whom you shine?

I am a herald of the lightning and a heavy drop from the cloud; but this lightning is called overman.

One must learn to love oneself with a sound and healthy love, so that one may bear to be with oneself.

The noble man wants to create new things and a new virtue; the good man wants the old things and that the old be preserved.

The good and the just call me the destroyer of morals; my tale is immoral, not moral.

Let your virtue be too exalted for the familiarity of names; if you must speak of it, do not be ashamed to stammer.

Beware of the good and the just! They like to crucify those who invent their own virtue for themselves.

All gods are poets' parables, poets' prevarications! Verily, it always lifts us higher—specifically to the realm of the clouds.

You higher men, the worst about you is that all of you have not learned to dance as one must dance—to dance beyond yourselves!

The great noon is when man stands at the midpoint of his path between animal and overman.

The Übermensch shall be the meaning of the earth! I beseech you, my brothers, remain faithful to the earth.

My death I praise to you, the free death which comes to me because I want it.

I would believe only in a god who could dance.

One repays a teacher badly if one always remains only a pupil.

The great events—they are not our loudest, but our stillest hours.

Not around the inventors of new noise, but around the inventors of new values does the world revolve; it revolves inaudibly.

You must have chaos within you to give birth to a dancing star.

Behold, I teach you the overman: he is this lightning, he is this madness!

The overman is the meaning of the earth. Let your will say: the overman shall be the meaning of the earth!

Beyond Good and Evil
The will to truth is really the will to power in disguise.

Philosophy is the personal confession of its author and a kind of involuntary memoir.

The fundamental faith of metaphysicians is the faith in opposite values.

There are no moral phenomena, only moral interpretations of phenomena.

Every great philosophy is the confession of its originator and a kind of involuntary autobiography.

The falseness of a judgment is not an objection to it; the question is how life-preserving it is.

Without granting the senses their validity, there is no green light for thinking at all.

The "subject" is a fiction; there is no "being" behind the doing, the "doer" is merely added to the deed.

At the origin of language and thought lies the fundamental error of equating truth with what is merely useful.

What is done out of love always occurs beyond good and evil.

The noble type of man feels himself as the determiner of values; he does not need approval.

Slave morality says "no" to what is "outside," "other," "not itself"; this "no" is its creative deed.

Master morality originates from a triumphant affirmation of oneself; slave morality from the outset says "No" to what is not itself.

The democratic movement is the heir of Christianity in its emphasis on herd morality and pity.

The greatest events are not our noisiest but our stillest hours.

The philosopher of the future will be a legislator, commanding values and saying, "Thus it shall be!"

There is no "truth for its own sake"; knowledge works as a tool of power.

Morality is merely sign-language, merely symptomatology; one must already know what it is about to derive profit from it.

A living thing seeks above all to discharge its strength—life itself is will to power.

The priest is the most dangerous kind of parasite, the great hater who poisons and slanders life.

The scholarly ox is not the ideal philosopher; philosophy requires command, creation, and will.

The "good man" of modern morality is the end-stage of the taming of the human beast.

We should not mistake the decline of a type for its necessary progress.

The free spirit is a relative concept; spirit frees itself, but to what is it bound before?

One must be a sea to receive a polluted stream without becoming unclean.

The genuine philosopher lives "unphilosophically" and "unwisely," above all imprudently.

In all philosophy, what has been silent must be heard; the real text is written between the lines.

The greatest part of conscious thought must still be ascribed to instinctive activity.

The "aim" of philosophy is not to find truth but to create a world in its own image.

We have no right to ask "who interprets?"; interpretation itself is a form of the will to power.

The "thing-in-itself" is nonsense; we relate to things only through our perspectives and needs.

The church is a tyranny of the sick over the healthy, built on a life-loathing psychology.

Woman's great art is the lie, her highest concern is appearance and beauty.

In the real world, contrary to Schopenhauer, Wille zur Macht (will to power) is the fundamental drive, not Wille zum Leben (will to life).

The saint is a monstrosity; we should use him to test our strength, not as an ideal.

To recognize untruth as a condition of life—that certainly means resisting accustomed value feelings.

A people is a detour of nature to get to six or seven great men.

The most spiritual men, as the strongest, find their happiness where others would find their destruction.

The noble soul has reverence for itself.

The man of knowledge must be able not only to love his enemies but also to hate his friends.

Everything deep loves a mask; the most profound things even hate the image and allegory.

One must learn to look away from oneself in order to see much.

The demand for "love of neighbor" is a sign of weakness and fear of one's own neighbor.

The great epochs of our life come when we gain the courage to rechristen our evil as our best.

The belief in a divine order of things is the naiveté of philosophers and the greatest regression in knowledge.

The greatest recent event—that "God is dead"—has yet to begin casting its shadows over Europe.

The philosopher must be a critic of morals, dissolving the habitual valuations that hold a people together.

Our virtues are determined by our spiritual crisis and sickness, not our health.

The "improvers" of mankind, like priests and philosophers, have really been the great calumniators of man.

One should part with life as Odysseus parted from Nausicaa—with a blessing, not in love.

On the Genealogy of Morality
The judgment "good" did not originate with those to whom "goodness" was shown but with the "good" themselves, the noble and powerful.

The priestly mode of valuation diverges from the knightly-aristocratic by being fundamentally reactive.

"Good and bad" and "good and evil" are two opposed pairs of values, originating from master and slave moralities respectively.

The slave revolt in morality begins when ressentiment itself becomes creative and gives birth to values.

Ressentiment is the poisonous, vengeful attitude of those who are powerless and cannot act directly.

The "evil" of slave morality is the primary concept, from which its positive counterpart "good" is derived as an afterthought.

The man of ressentiment lies in wait for his enemy in a spiritual rather than physical sense.

The "good man" of Christian morality is the same as the "evil man" of noble morality, just renamed.

The ascetic ideal springs from the protective instinct of a degenerating life that is fighting for its existence.

Punishment was not invented to produce a feeling of guilt; it originally had nothing to do with inner repentance.

The feeling of guilt, the "bad conscience," is the result of the internalization of man's instinct for freedom and aggression.

The entire inner world of man was expanded when the discharge of instincts outward was blocked by the constraints of society.

The "bad conscience" is a sickness, but as pregnancy is a sickness, promising a new creation.

The concept of God as the ultimate creditor allowed the feeling of guilt to reach its most terrible and sublime heights.

The ascetic priest is the shepherd of this sick flock, exploiting the bad conscience for his own power.

The ascetic ideal gives meaning to suffering by interpreting it as deserved punishment.

The will to truth is the latest and noblest form of the ascetic ideal.

The ascetic ideal has been the only meaning offered for human suffering so far; man would rather will nothingness than not will.

The philosopher's will to truth is a form of the ascetic will to deny the apparent, sensual world.

The "free will" and "responsible subject" were invented to make mankind calculable and to justify punishment.

The sovereign individual, master of a free will, is the ripe fruit of the long history of breeding a creature that can promise.

To breed an animal with the right to make promises is the central task of morality.

Mnemotechnics, the art of memory, was not gentle but involved blood, torture, and sacrifice to burn memories in.

The origins of morality are steeped in blood and horror, not benevolence and reason.

Justice originally stemmed from the perspective of active, strong, spontaneous parties, not from reactive ressentiment.

The idea of "equality before God" is the most poisonous fruit of the tree of Christian morality.

Modern historical scholarship is the servant of the ascetic ideal, as it seeks truth at any cost, even at the cost of life.

The scientific atheist who clings to the unconditional will to truth is still a pious man, still operating under the ascetic ideal.

The "subject" is a fiction added to the deed; there is no "being" behind the doing.

The priest is the direction-changer of ressentiment, who gives the herd a target for its bottled-up aggression.

The saint is merely the self-deification of the sick herd animal who has succeeded in making his weakness sacred.

The value of the "unegoistic," the instincts of pity and self-denial, is the value of the herd.

The "last man" who blinks and seeks comfort is the logical conclusion of the herd morality.

The meaning of the ascetic ideal is that the will would rather will nothingness than not will at all.

The democratic movement is the heir of the Christian movement, continuing its leveling, herd-animal morality.

The "improvers" of mankind have actually made mankind sicker and more decadent.

The "good man" is the final stage of the tamed human animal, rendered harmless and predictable.

The philosopher must be a psychologist and a genealogist, digging beneath moral concepts to find their ignoble origins.

Our modern "guilt" is a secularized form of the religious concept of "sin before God."

The entire moral edifice rests on the fundamental error of confusing cause and effect.

The noble man honors himself as powerful and also as having power over himself.

The slave's morality is essentially a morality of utility, where "good" means "useful to me, the suffering one."

The "blond beast" is not a racial concept but a psychological image of the buried, latent instinct for freedom and aggression.

The state was born not from a social contract but from a violent conquest by a pack of blond beasts.

The genealogical method reveals that concepts have a history and change their meaning based on power struggles.

The "bad conscience" is a profound illness, but one that made man interesting and capable of depth.

Pity is not a virtue but a narcotic for the suffering, prolonging misery and multiplying it.

The will to power can turn against itself in the form of the bad conscience, creating an inner landscape of self-torture.

We need a critique of moral values; the value of these values themselves must first be called into question.

The Case of Wagner
Richard Wagner is a great corrupter of music, a neurosis who has made music sick.

Wagner's art is decadent; it appeals to our exhaustion and fascination with decay.

Wagner is an actor, not a true musician; his genius lies in theatrical effect, not in profound musical structure.

Wagner's music is not German; it is a European, decadent phenomenon that has corrupted taste.

Wagner flatters every nihilistic instinct and disguises it as redemption.

Wagner is the modern artist par excellence, the Cagliostro of modernity who seduces the masses.

Wagner's music is heavy, humid, and oppressive; it lacks the cheerful lightness and Mediterranean clarity of true art.

Wagner's heroes and heroines are hysterics, not noble characters; they are studies in neurophysiology.

Wagner invented the "infinite melody" to flatter the unmusical and to create a sense of endless, aimless longing.

Wagner's art is an art of effect; he calculates everything for the audience's nervous system, not for the spirit.

Wagner's music is brutal and artificial; it overwhelms the senses rather than elevating them.

The Wagnerian "redemption" is a false redemption, a longing for nothingness and extinction.

Wagner corrupts his orchestra, turning it into a giant, roaring beast rather than an instrument of nuance.

Wagner is a miniaturist disguised as a monumentalist; his apparent grandeur is built from tiny, obsessive details.

Wagner's music is democratic; it appeals to the masses because it is obvious, exaggerated, and works on the nerves.

Bizet's Carmen represents the antithesis of Wagner: it is light, cheerful, southern, and free from German heaviness.

The Wagnerian Gesamtkunstwerk (total work of art) is a tyrannical concept that subordinates all arts to a theatrical dictatorship.

Wagner's art makes one sick; Bizet's art makes one more healthy and grateful for life.

Wagner's success is a sign of the general decline of European culture and the victory of herd instincts.

Wagner is the great seducer; he makes what is decadent and sick seem profound and desirable.

Wagner's "ideas" are second-rate philosopher's ideas, borrowed from Schopenhauer and dressed up in music drama.

The Wagnerian cult is a religion for atheists, a form of theatrical piety.

Wagner has made music into a means of acting, of gesticulating, rather than a pure art of sound.

The future belongs to the anti-Wagnerian, the musician who is subtle, cheerful, and profound in a different way.

I find Wagner's music impossible to breathe in; it stifles the free spirit.

Wagner is a problem only because he is a sickness; he must be studied as a physician studies a disease.

Wagner's art exhausts; true art should invigorate and increase one's feeling of power.

The leitmotif is a childish invention, a musical signpost for the simple-minded who cannot follow drama without labels.

Wagner is essentially inhuman; his characters are abstractions, not living beings.

The Bayreuth Festival is a monument to German self-admiration and cultural arrogance.

Wagner's music is the antithesis of Goethe's ideal of classical clarity and measure.

To recover from Wagner, one must turn to the music that is the opposite of all German ideals: the music of Bizet.

Wagner represents the theatricalization of art, its descent into mere spectacle and sensation.

The modern soul finds its most accurate expression in Wagner's vibrating, uncertain, chaotic music.

Wagner is the great symptom of our cultural exhaustion, not its cure.

I was the first to see through Wagner's pose, to recognize the actor behind the mask of the genius.

Wagner's "infinite melody" is a means to hide his poverty of real melodic invention.

Wagner's art is an art of lying; it convinces us that we are experiencing something profound when we are merely being stimulated.

The triumph of Wagner is the triumph of the stage manager over the musician.

Wagner's music is dangerous; it undermines health and clarity of thought.

The pathos of Wagner is always false; it is a contrived, overblown emotion.

Wagner corrupted even Nietzsche himself for a time, which is why he must now wage war against him.

Wagner's art is the perfect expression of the romantic ideal, which is a form of sickness and world-weariness.

To understand Wagner is to understand the modern, for he concentrated all its ambiguities and sicknesses in himself.

One must have loved Wagner to have the right to criticize him; otherwise, the criticism is empty.

I oppose Wagner because his art is the enemy of life, a seduction to decadence.

Wagner is a tyrant; his will to dominate the audience is boundless.

The philosopher's task is to be a physician of culture, and diagnosing Wagner is a crucial case study.

Wagner's music is essentially literary; it is music in the service of a drama, not music for its own sake.

### **From *The Antichrist* (1888)**

1.  **Christianity is a *slave morality*:** It originated from the *ressentiment* (resentment) of the weak against the noble, strong, and life-affirming.
2.  **It inverts natural values:** It calls the healthy "sinful," the strong "evil," and privileges the poor, sick, and meek.
3.  **The figure of the priest is parasitic:** The priest thrives by diagnosing humanity as "sinful" and then offering himself as the sole cure.
4.  **The concept of "God" is decadent:** The Christian God is a declaration of war against life, nature, and the will to live.
5.  **The "Holy Scriptures" are corrupt:** The Bible is not divine revelation but a compilation of priestly fabrications to secure power.
6.  **"Faith" is intellectual cowardice:** It requires the closing of one's eyes to evidence and reason; it is *not* a virtue.
7.  **Christianity is *anti-nature*:** It makes an ideal out of opposing natural instincts (sexuality, ambition, pride).
8.  **The "afterlife" is a nihilistic invention:** The belief in heaven devalues *this* world, the only world that exists.
9.  **Compassion (*Mitleid*) is a dangerous virtue:** It multiplies suffering, preserves what is suffering, and weakens the strong.
10. **Jesus was misunderstood:** The "glad tidings" of Jesus were a practice of living *outside* all dogma, in a state of inner peace ("kingdom of God" within).
11. **Paul was the true founder of Christianity:** He institutionalized Jesus' death as a sacrificial dogma, creating a religion of ressentiment.
12. **Christianity is *Platonism for the masses*:** It peddles a two-world theory (true world vs. apparent world) to devalue earthly existence.
13. **It is the greatest misfortune of humanity:** Its success represents the victory of the weak herd over the potential higher types.
14. **It is responsible for the corruption of reason:** It elevated "faith" above intellectual honesty and scientific inquiry.
15. **The Christian ideal is a denial of beauty and nobility:** It glorifies what is pitiable, ugly, and failed.
16. **It is a religion of *pity*:** It creates a culture where suffering becomes a badge of honor and a tool for manipulation.
17. **It opposes every natural hierarchy:** It makes a virtue of leveling, of bringing the high down to the low.
18. **It invented "sin" as an attack on humanity:** The concept turns natural human drives into sources of guilt and self-loathing.
19. **Its morality is one of *unconditional obedience*:** It demands submission, not strength or self-overcoming.
20. **It has no connection to reality:** Its dogmas (sin, redemption, final judgment) are pure fiction with no factual basis.
21. **It is the enemy of the *will to power*:** It preaches humility and self-renunciation, the antithesis of the life force.
22. **The Church is the *anti-type* of a healthy institution:** It is a conspiracy against health, beauty, happiness, and courage.
23. **It turned Dionysian values upside-down:** Where Dionysus represents ecstatic affirmation of life, Christianity preaches ascetic denial.
24. **It must be abolished, not reformed:** Its very foundation is poisonous; it requires not critique, but active destruction.
25. **I write against it as a *physician of culture*:** Diagnosing Christianity is the first step toward recovering the health of European humanity.

### **From *Ecce Homo* (1888)**

1.  **"How One Becomes What One Is":** The book is an experiment in self-overcoming and understanding the fate that made him *Nietzsche*.
2.  **A declaration of greatness:** It is a conscious, provocative act of self-celebration to force a revaluation of the scale by which we measure value.
3.  **A testament to perfect health:** Written at the peak of his lucidity, it embodies the *affirmative* spirit he championed.
4.  **Justification of his life's task:** His mission was to complete nihilism and inaugurate a revaluation of all values.
5.  **Genealogy of his ideas:** He explains how his personal physiology, habits, and tastes (diet, climate, reading) shaped his philosophy.
6.  **Why I Am So Wise:** His wisdom comes from experiencing extremes (sickness and health), which gave him a multidimensional perspective.
7.  **The concept of *amor fati* (love of fate):** The formula for his greatness is saying "Yes" to all of his life, without exception.
8.  **Attack on German culture:** He disavows the "Reich," German beer, music (after Wagner), and its intellectual heaviness.
9.  **Defense of his polemics:** His attacks are not personal but "conditions of life," using a "hammer" to test idols (convictions).
10. **Why I Am So Clever:** His cleverness is in instinctual choices—selecting the right nourishment, people, climate, and recreation.
11. **The revaluation of all values is *my* task:** He positions himself as a world-historical force, a "destiny."
12. **Explanation of his solitude:** His isolation was necessary, as he was posthumous, speaking to a future audience.
13. **On his books:** He provides chapter-by-chapter guides to *Zarathustra*, *Beyond Good and Evil*, etc., as keys to understanding him.
14. ***Zarathustra* is his greatest gift:** It is the highest book ever written, a dithyramb to life, containing the idea of the Eternal Recurrence.
15. **The psychologist's advantage:** He claims unparalleled psychological insight, able to uncover the base motives behind "idealistic" systems.
16. **The *Dionysian* as ultimate affirmation:** He identifies himself with the god Dionysus, the spirit of tragic joy and life-affirmation.
17. **War against *ressentiment*:** He declares his instinct has been entirely free of the reactive, vengeful feeling that defines slave morality.
18. **Praise of contradiction:** He contains opposites within himself (skepticism and passion, criticality and creativity) without conflict.
19. **His relation to Wagner:** A story of profound influence and even deeper betrayal—Wagner's descent into Christian decadence.
20. **The concept of the *Übermensch*:** The meaning of the earth, a being who creates new values beyond good and evil.
21. **The Eternal Recurrence as the supreme test:** The thought that one must will the eternal repetition of one's life as the ultimate Yes.
22. **I am not a man, I am dynamite:** A declaration of his explosive, earth-shattering philosophical impact.
23. **Attack on "idealism":** All idealists are liars because they deny the basic, earthly, instinctual conditions of life.
24. **His readers must be prepared:** He writes for the very few, those with courage, intellectual conscience, and free spirits.
25. **"Have I been understood?":** The final, defiant question, marking the line between those stuck in the old morality and those ready for the new.
