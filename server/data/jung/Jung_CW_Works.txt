**Psychology and Alchemy (CW 12)**

1. Alchemy is the historical counterpart of our modern psychology of the unconscious.
2. The so-called “cheap” philosophical gold is not to be understood chemically, but morally and spiritually.
3. The process of individuation is often mirrored in the stages of the alchemical *opus*.
4. The *lapis philosophorum* is a symbol of the self.
5. The union of opposites (*coniunctio*) is a central aim of both alchemy and psychic development.
6. Dreams and active imagination can produce images identical to alchemical symbols.
7. The alchemist’s projection of unconscious contents gave substance to the chemical process.
8. The transformation of the personality is akin to the transmutation of base metal into gold.
9. The *prima materia* is both the chaotic initial substance and the unconscious psyche itself.
10. The mandala is a symbol of wholeness and appears in both alchemy and dreams.

**Alchemical Studies (CW 13)**

1. Mercurius is the duplex spiritus, both material and spiritual, and the personification of the unconscious.
2. The spirit Mercurius is a trickster figure and a psychopomp.
3. The philosophical tree is a symbol of the developmental process of the psyche.
4. Alchemical symbolism represents a continuation of the Gnostic worldview.
5. The *arbor philosophica* often depicts the union of opposites in its imagery.
6. The alchemical vessel is the container for the transformation process, analogous to the psychic field of the individuating person.
7. The water of the alchemists is a symbol of the unconscious.
8. The goal of the opus is the production of the incorruptible, eternal body, the *corpus glorificationis*.
9. Paracelsus’s ideas are a important link between late medieval thought and modern psychology.
10. The figure of the Anthropos is a recurring symbol of psychic wholeness.

**The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious (CW 9i)**

1. The collective unconscious is a substratum common to all humanity, consisting of archetypes.
2. Archetypes are innate, universal psychic predispositions that form the substrate of all human experience.
3. The archetype in itself is empty and purely formal, a possibility of representation.
4. The mother archetype manifests in countless symbolic forms, from the personal mother to the Church.
5. The process of individuation involves a confrontation with archetypal figures, such as the shadow and the anima/animus.
6. The child archetype represents futurity, potential, and the synthesis of opposites.
7. The archetype of rebirth is one of the most important analytic concepts.
8. The self is the archetype of order, wholeness, and the totality of the psyche.
9. Archetypes are not inherited ideas, but inherited *modes of functioning*.
10. The motif of the "treasure hard to attain" is an archetypal image for the value of the self.

**Psychology and Religion: West and East (CW 11)**

1. A religion is a careful observation of what Rudolf Otto called the *numinosum*.
2. The modern individual must find an individual religious outlook, not rely solely on collective dogma.
3. The symbols of the Christian Mass represent a process of psychic transformation.
4. The idea of the Trinity is a profound archetypal symbol, but it lacks the element of darkness or evil.
5. The Eastern mind emphasizes the cosmic and impersonal, while the Western mind focuses on the personal and historical.
6. Yoga practices, if adopted by Westerners without psychological understanding, can lead to dangerous dissociations.
7. The *mandala* is a symbol of the self used in both Eastern meditation and spontaneous Western fantasy.
8. The Zen *koan* aims to break the grip of the conscious, rational mind to allow a new psychic perspective.
9. The central Christian symbol of Christ is an image of the self, representing totality and the union of opposites.
10. A psychological understanding of religious symbols does not reduce their meaning, but reveals their living function.

**Civilization in Transition (CW 10)**

1. The individual's feeling of moral isolation and powerlessness in the face of the mass is the modern neurosis.
2. The great danger of our time is the dissolution of the individual in the mass psyche.
3. The state is a blind force that cannot replace individual moral responsibility.
4. The true basis of any state is the psychological individual, not the anonymous mass.
5. The present world situation reflects a psychic epidemic of gigantic proportions.
6. The European war (WWI) was a catastrophic outbreak of the collective unconscious.
7. The root of political mass movements lies in the psychic dissociation of the modern individual.
8. The only real and effective protection against collective forces is individual spiritual and moral strength.
9. The fate of the world depends on the change of consciousness in the individual.
10. The doctor is unavoidably involved in the social and political questions of the day.

**The Spirit in Man, Art, and Literature (CW 15)**

1. The work of art is not a personal product, but a collective one arising from the unconscious.
2. The creative process has a feminine quality and is characterized by a surrender of conscious will.
3. The poet or artist is the collective mouthpiece of the unconscious psyche of mankind.
4. Picasso's art reveals a descent into the chaotic depths of the unconscious, a fragmentation of modern sensibility.
5. *Ulysses* by James Joyce is a representation of the cool, objective, disintegrating modern consciousness.
6. The *Nekyia*, or night-sea journey, is a archetypal motif in creativity, a descent for the purpose of renewal.
7. True creativity springs from the autonomous complex, not the personal ego.
8. The psychological analysis of art must follow a reductive method to the personal, and a constructive method to the archetypal.
9. The primordial vision in art is a raw, overwhelming experience of the collective unconscious.
10. The meaning of a great work of art is not explained by the artist's personal psychology.

**Freud and Psychoanalysis (CW 4)**

1. Freud's theory of repression is of fundamental importance for understanding neuroses.
2. The theory of infantile sexuality is a necessary, though one-sided, viewpoint.
3. Psychoanalysis, as Freud understood it, is a reductive method that traces everything back to primitive instinctual wishes.
4. The transference is the central phenomenon of analytical treatment.
5. My main difference with Freud concerns the nature of the libido, which I see as a general psychic energy, not solely sexual.
6. The Oedipus complex is not the primary cause of neurosis, but is often itself a symptom of parental influence.
7. The unconscious is not merely a repository of repressed personal contents, but has a creative, forward-looking function.
8. A purely causal-reductive view destroys the meaning and value of psychic contents for the individual.
9. Freud's emphasis on sexuality as the sole driving force is too narrow.
10. The analyst must be aware of his own personality and prejudices, for they influence the treatment.

**Psychiatric Studies (CW 1)**

1. The feeling-toned complex is the architect of dreams and the source of neurotic symptoms.
2. So-called occult phenomena are often manifestations of unconscious complexes.
3. Dementia praecox (schizophrenia) involves a dissociation of consciousness and an autonomous functioning of psychic fragments.
4. The word-association experiment provides an objective method for detecting unconscious complexes.
5. In paranoid conditions, unconscious complexes are projected onto the environment.
6. The psyche is an energetic system subject to laws of equivalence and entropy.
7. The psychogenesis of schizophrenia must be acknowledged, even if organic factors are also present.
8. Hysterical symptoms are symbolic representations of an unconscious conflict.
9. The personality of the doctor is a crucial therapeutic factor.
10. Careful clinical observation is the indispensable foundation of all psychological theory.

**Experimental Researches (CW 2)**

1. The delayed reaction time in word-association tests indicates the presence of a feeling-toned complex.
2. Complexes behave like autonomous partial personalities within the psyche.
3. The galvanic psychophysical response is an objective measure of emotional arousal related to complexes.
4. Perseveration in responses shows the continued activity of a complex.
5. The method of word association allows for the differential diagnosis of psychiatric conditions.
6. Complexes have a constellating power, attracting related ideas and memories.
7. Faulty reactions (e.g., repetitions, slips) in the test are not random but determined by the complex.
8. The emotional charge of a complex can influence memory and judgment.
9. The experiment reveals that the ego is not the only center of the psyche.
10. Statistical analysis of reaction times and responses provides empirical evidence for the theory of complexes.

**The Undiscovered Self (1957)**

1. The central, fear-laden problem of our age is the threat to the individual from the overpowering collectives.
2. The state and society are seen as concrete realities, while the individual is a mere abstraction.
3. The real life of the collective consists in the interrelations of its individual members.
4. The individual's only protection is individual psychological understanding.
5. Mass-mindedness is the suffocation of the individual moral decision by numbers and statistics.
6. The Christian doctrine of *gnosis* is a recognition of the individual's inner knowledge of God.
7. The world situation is the reflection of a psychic condition in modern man.
8. The change must begin with the individual, with a recognition of the shadow and the contravening will of the self.
9. Psychology, as the science of the soul, is destined to play a mediating role in the modern crisis.
10. The statist superstructure is an accretion that has nothing to do with the real nature of man.

**Flying Saucers: A Modern Myth (1958)**

1. UFOs are firstly a psychological phenomenon; their physical reality is a secondary question.
2. The rumour of flying saucers is a modern vision, a living myth in the making.
3. The circular form of the saucer is a classic symbol of wholeness, a mandala.
4. The phenomenon expresses an emotional tension and a psychic distress of our age.
5. It is a projection of a psychic content, an archetype of order, appearing in the sky.
6. The saucer symbolizes a synthesis of opposites, a union of consciousness and the unconscious.
7. The phenomenon compensates for the one-sided, rationalistic spirit of our time.
8. It represents a longing for salvation in an age threatened by total destruction.
9. The collective anxiety of the Cold War period provides the energy for this projection.
10. The myth shows that archetypal contents are not dead but can appear in contemporary form.

**On the Nature of the Psyche (1947)**

1. The psyche is a self-regulating system that maintains its equilibrium like the body.
2. Consciousness is a late-born descendant of the unconscious psyche.
3. The unconscious is not merely reactive, but is spontaneously productive.
4. The archetype is psychoid, i.e., it functions beyond the psychic realm, on the frontier of matter.
5. Instinct and archetype are the two poles of a psychoid reality.
6. The psyche is a reality *sui generis*, not merely an epiphenomenon of brain chemistry.
7. The principle of synchronicity must be considered alongside causality as a factor in explanation.
8. The unconscious possesses a field of perception independent of the sensory functions.
9. The psyche is transformative energy.
10. The goal of psychic development is the realization of the self, the archetype of wholeness.

**Seven Sermons to the Dead (1916)**

1. The dead came back from Jerusalem, where they found not what they sought.
2. The pleroma is the nothingness and fullness, the beginning and end of all created things.
3. In the pleroma there is no differentiation, and all qualities are balanced and void.
4. Created beings are distinct from the pleroma, yet the pleroma is within them.
5. Distinctiveness is the essence of the creature, achieved through its own quality.
6. Your own quality is your distinctiveness, your being, your god and your devil.
7. Abraxas is the god who is both creator and destroyer, light and darkness.
8. The sun-god is the principle of force and endurance, the father-god.
9. The devil is the other half of the godhead, the principle of negation and reversal.
10. Man is a gateway through which the outer world of the gods and demons passes into the inner world.

**The Tavistock Lectures (1935)**

1. The psyche is an object of inner experience, not merely a conceptual abstraction.
2. The word-association experiment demonstrates the reality of feeling-toned complexes.
3. Dreams are not mere wish-fulfillments but are natural, spontaneous products of the unconscious.
4. The dream is a self-portrait of the actual psychic situation.
5. The unconscious has a compensatory function relative to consciousness.
6. The archetypes are the instinct's perception of itself, or the self-portrait of the instinct.
7. The persona is the individual's system of adaptation to the world, a mask of the collective psyche.
8. The anima is the personification of the unconscious in a man, a figure that connects him to the collective unconscious.
9. The transcendent function arises from the union of conscious and unconscious contents.
10. The goal of analysis is to achieve a vital connection between the ego and the unconscious.

**Visions Seminars**

1. These visions are not a pathological product, but a creative functioning of the unconscious.
2. The images should be understood symbolically, not literally.
3. The figure of the "Anima" appears as a guide to the inner world.
4. The process depicted is an involuntary, spontaneous process of individuation.
5. The mountain often symbolizes the goal, the place of transformation.
6. The serpent is a symbol of renewal and of the unconscious instinctual force.
7. The colors in the visions have specific symbolic values (e.g., black for the unconscious, red for passion/energy).
8. The "inner Christ" or "Philosopher's Stone" appears as a symbol of the attained self.
9. The sequence of visions follows an archetypal pattern of death and rebirth.
10. Active imagination is the method by which such psychic material can be engaged and integrated.

**Dream Analysis Seminars**

1. The dream is a fact; it is the primary datum from which we must start.
2. Never apply a theory to a dream; let the dream speak for itself.
3. The first rule is: always take the dream literally, as a conscious statement.
4. The dream is a natural event; it is not clever or deceitful, it simply *is*.
5. The dream compensates the attitude of the conscious mind.
6. The dream always emphasizes the point of view of the unconscious.
7. To understand a dream, you must establish its context—the conscious situation of the dreamer.
8. Amplification by means of parallels (mythology, folklore, etc.) is essential for interpreting archetypal motifs.
9. Series of dreams are far more instructive than single dreams.
10. The ultimate goal of dream interpretation is not just understanding, but a change in the dreamer's attitude.
